The Digital Product Passport (DPP) is a mandatory digital record under the EU’s Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR), designed to collect data on a product's material composition, environmental footprint, repairability, and supply chain. Accessible via a QR code, it aims to enhance sustainability, traceability, and circularity, heavily affecting textiles, batteries, and electronics.

Key Aspects of the Digital Product Passport (DPP):
Objective: To support the EU's goal of a circular economy by providing transparency on a product's sustainability and helping consumers and businesses make informed decisions.
Data Included: The passport covers materials, chemical components, carbon footprint, reparability, durability, and end-of-life instructions.
Affected Sectors: Nearly all physical products sold in the EU will eventually need a DPP, with early implementation targeting high-impact sectors like batteries (2027), textiles, consumer electronics, and construction materials.
Implementation & Timing: The central registry is expected to launch around July 2026, with mandatory battery passports beginning in early 2027.
Accessibility: The data will be accessible to consumers, businesses, and authorities, typically via a QR code or similar identifier placed on the product.

How it Works:
Unique Identifier: Every product or batch will have a unique digital ID.
Information Sharing: Suppliers and manufacturers must upload data at each step of the product's life cycle, from raw material extraction to production.
Transparency: It helps businesses comply with environmental regulations and provides consumers with information on product durability.
